Speakers (provisonal)

Fred 'Pappy' Raynal

Fred Raynal, PhD, is the founder and CEO of QUARKSLAB. Previously, he worked 3 years at EADS, including working as a core member of EADS IW, then created the SOGETI ESEC R&D (lab) team he managed for 5 years. He also is founder of the french conference SSTIC and magazine MISC. Beside "founding", he enjoys both technical hacking, information warfare and finding ways to combine both in order to find different (and hopefully better) ways to do information security.

Emilien Girault

Emilien Girault is a security auditor and pentester at ANSSI (French Network and Information Security Agency). His main fields of interest include vulnerability exploitation, cryptography and reverse engineering of PLC firmwares and client-server applications. He enjoys participating in various security challenges, such as SSTIC and NoSuchCon. He's the author of several articles published on old blogs like Ghosts In The Stack and Sogeti ESEC, as well as DOAR-E and French magazine MISC.

Axel '0vercl0k' Souchet

Axel Souchet is a fresh CS graduate who recently joined Microsoft and the MSRC team as a security software engineer. He basically loves computers, challenges, and low level subjects: bug hunting, code-obfuscation, weird machines, etc. He is also not really good at writing Bio as you can see.

Jonathan Salwan

Jonathan Salwan is a junior security researcher at Quarkslab who has a strong interest in low-level computing and program analysis. His research is mostly focused on both static and dynamic program analysis.

Mariano 'Emdel' Graziano

Mariano Graziano is currently a Ph.D. student at the Software and System Security group of Institut Eurecom in Sophia-Antipolis. In his thesis, he tries to protect the world by developing new automated techniques to analyze malware and to defeat chaos in physical memory dumps.

Aurélien 'Dad' Wailly

Aurélien Wailly is an engineer in cloud computing security at Orange Labs. His primary research interest is the application of autonomic concepts to adapt security to cloud environments. He also studies underlying virtualization security to lower attack surface and provide remediation.

Samuel Chevet

Samuel Chevet is a former member of the EPITA Systems Laboratory (LSE). After his graduation, he joined, as a junior security researcher, the Sogeti ESEC R&D lab. His interests mainly lie in reverse engineering, vulnerability research and windows internals. He also likes to study binary protections on his spare time.

Guy 'Shift'

Guy is a freelance security researcher, he likes low level things, vulnerability research and reverse engineering. He has been wandering around in the field of security for the past several years drifting from side of the coin to the other from statically reversing software into breaking different software solutions. There's not much to say about him other than that he likes hypervisors, static reversing engineering and breaking all the things.

Florian 'Mysterie' Ledoux

Mr. Florian Ledoux (@Myst3rie) is a security researcher at Vupen. Mr Ledoux has been a team member of the EADS IW security team for 2 years. He is interested in producing ongoing cutting-edge research in the area of application and operating system security. Passionate of fuzzing, vulnerability discovery and analysis. Mr Ledoux also has authored white papers about dropbox security and was invited to present his work at HACK.LU and APPSEC.CH conferences. He also authored analysis of windows vulnerabilities on the french magazine MISC and on his blog mysterie.fr .

Short descriptions

Breaking the Cube - Guy 'Shift'

Nowadays virtualization is used as an isolation mechanism to provide the ability to separate one OS from another, while this might presumably fulfill its cause faithfully, the design and implementation of the software which runs and manages those OSes, the VMM, sometime might by faulty. This imposes a false curtain by making corporations think that Hypervisors by default provide security and isolation without any costs, letting their users trust their own Hypervisor to provide them isolation and security at any cost. This talk shows the history of a few interesting bugs which were patched in the passed year by the Xen Security Team I will also try to shed some light on the attack surface Xen offers for people who are willing to enter the trek of exploiting hypervisors.
